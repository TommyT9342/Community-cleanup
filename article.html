Why Keeping Your Community Clean Actually Matters

West Bedford Student Community Clean Up  ·  West Bedford School

A clean community isn't just about looks. It's about responsibility, shared pride, and the kind of place you want to grow up in. This is why a group of West Bedford students decided to do something about it — and why we think everyone else should too.

This Is Everyone's Responsibility

Think about the streets you walk every day. The park where you hang out after school. The areas around West Bedford that you know by heart. Now imagine them covered in litter — packaging, cigarette butts, wrappers left behind by people who didn't think it was their problem.

Here's the thing: it is everyone's problem. A community only stays clean when the people who live in it treat it with care. That's not just the job of the municipality or a cleaning crew — it starts with ordinary people deciding that where they live is worth looking after.

That's exactly why we chose community cleaning as our project. It's something real, something achievable, and something that directly improves the place we call home. Unlike bigger, more complex problems, this one is completely within our reach. With a solid plan, the right tools, and enough students willing to show up, we can make a visible difference in six weeks.

The Problem: When Nobody Cares, It Shows

The hard truth is that a lot of people don't think littering is a big deal. They drop a wrapper, walk away, and assume someone else will deal with it. Multiply that attitude by hundreds of people, every single day, and you get a community that slowly deteriorates — not because of one major incident, but because of a thousand small acts of indifference.

And the damage is real. Litter attracts more litter — research consistently shows that people are more likely to litter in an area that's already littered. It's a cycle that feeds itself. Once neglect sets in, it becomes the norm, and that's when neighbourhoods start to feel unsafe, unwelcoming, and forgotten.

Everyone in the community is impacted by this — and everyone benefits when it changes. There's no group of people who would prefer to live in a dirtier neighbourhood. Which means the only barrier to change is getting people to actually act.

What the Data Tells Us — Right Here in Nova Scotia

This isn't just a general problem. In Nova Scotia specifically, litter on provincial roadways has been studied, and the breakdown is striking:

Source: The Great Nova Scotia Pick-Me-Up & NS Adopt-A-Highway Program

Nearly 90% of the litter on our roadways comes from just three sources. That actually makes the problem more solvable — if we know where litter comes from, we can target those sources directly. Awareness signs near fast food restaurants, more accessible public trash cans, and education around proper disposal could meaningfully reduce that number before anyone picks up a single bag.

Nova Scotia already has programs in place to address this. The Great Nova Scotia Pick-Me-Up and the Adopt-A-Highway Program organize volunteer-driven litter clean-ups across the province, giving groups exactly like ours the framework to take action in our own area.

Three Ways People See This Issue

Not everyone thinks about community cleanliness the same way. Here are the three most common perspectives — and why two of them are missing the point.

✔ Perspective 1 — Take Action

The most straightforward view: everyone pitches in, cleans up, and makes the community a better place. The more people who get involved, the bigger the impact. This is the approach behind our student cleanup — divide the area, assign sections, and get it done together.

✘ Perspective 2 — It Doesn't Matter

Some people feel that cleaning up after others isn't their responsibility, or that the effort isn't worth it. This mindset is exactly what allows communities to deteriorate. The problem doesn't solve itself — and the longer it's ignored, the worse it gets.

◆ Perspective 3 — Prevent, Don't Just React

A third view argues that instead of cleaning up litter, we should stop creating it in the first place — through better habits, recycling awareness, and being mindful about waste. This is the most sustainable long-term approach, and it works best alongside active cleanup efforts, not instead of them.

The reality is that all three perspectives have a role to play. Cleaning up addresses what's already there. Prevention stops it from coming back. And getting people to care — changing the "it doesn't matter" mindset — is what makes both of those things sustainable over time.

What a Cleaner Community Actually Looks Like

When a neighbourhood is clean and well-maintained, something shifts. People feel safer. They take more pride in where they live. They're more likely to maintain it, because a clean space signals that it's cared for and worth caring about. Local businesses benefit. Green spaces become actually usable. Kids can play outside without navigating around trash.

The Halifax Regional Municipality recognized this, releasing a Comprehensive Litter Strategy in January 2026 that outlines a regional plan across four areas: community engagement and marketing, waste infrastructure, property standards, and enforcement. The key goals include ensuring businesses keep their surrounding areas clean, and fixing litter at the source rather than just cleaning it up after the fact.

What We Can Actually Do

Big strategies and government plans matter — but so does what happens at the neighbourhood level. Here's what we, as West Bedford students, can realistically do right now:

Our Plan of Action

  • Organize a student-led clean up of West Bedford before the end of the school year — divide the area into sections so the work is manageable for everyone
  • Gather the right tools: gloves, bags, and grabbers so students can clean safely and efficiently
  • Spread awareness through this site and word of mouth — getting more people involved multiplies the impact
  • Push for better access to public trash cans in areas where litter tends to pile up
  • Encourage people to think before they litter — especially near fast food spots, which account for nearly half of all roadway litter in Nova Scotia

None of this is complicated. It just requires people deciding it's worth doing — and then actually showing up. That's the whole point. You don't need special skills or experience. You just need to care about where you live.
